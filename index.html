About This Project

What problem this solves, how the pipeline works, and what the results mean in plain terms.

The Problem

The Asia-Pacific region has dozens of overlapping Free Trade Agreements. Each contains thousands of legal provisions covering tariffs, customs rules, investment protections, and dispute resolution. For a trade analyst or customs officer, understanding how three different agreements treat the same topic — say, Rules of Origin — normally requires days of manual reading and cross-referencing.

This project automates that comparison using Large Language Models. It reads the full text of three ASEAN-centred FTAs, breaks each into individual provisions, classifies each into a policy category, and generates structured cross-agreement comparisons — reducing analyst triage from weeks to hours.

RCEP

Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership. 15 nations. Signed 2020. The world's largest FTA by trade volume. Covers goods, services, investment, IP, and dispute settlement.

2,171 provisions · 53.5% of corpus

AHKFTA

ASEAN–Hong Kong Free Trade Agreement. Signed 2017. Goods-focused by design — no services, investment, or dispute settlement chapters. References WTO rules instead.

362 provisions · 8.9% of corpus

AANZFTA

ASEAN–Australia–New Zealand FTA. Signed 2009. Comprehensive like RCEP — includes investment (Ch.11) and its own dispute settlement mechanism.

1,526 provisions · 37.6% of corpus

How the Pipeline Works

Step 1 — Extraction. Agreement PDFs are read using a three-step fallback: first a layout-aware reader (pdfplumber), then a lower-level text extractor (PyMuPDF), then Optical Character Recognition (OCR) for scanned pages. Text is split into individual provisions — the smallest meaningful legal unit.

Step 2 — Embedding. Every provision is converted into a numerical vector using a sentence embedding model (MiniLM). These vectors are stored in a database (ChromaDB) so that semantically similar provisions can be retrieved quickly without exact keyword matching.

Step 3 — Classification. Two LLMs (LLaMA 3.3 70B and Qwen 3 32B) classify each provision into one of 11 policy categories. Each model is tested under three prompt strategies: zero-shot (no examples), few-shot (two labelled examples), and chain-of-thought (step-by-step reasoning). A stratified sample of 100 provisions per agreement is used so that the larger RCEP corpus does not dominate results.

Step 4 — RAG Comparison. For each policy category, the three most relevant provisions from each agreement are retrieved (nine total) and given to the LLM to write a structured comparison. This is more reliable than single-provision classification because the model reads primary text before responding.

Step 5 — Validation. 50 provisions are hand-labelled by the author and used to score all six model-strategy combinations on accuracy and Macro-F1.

What the Numbers Mean

The best classification accuracy in this project is 48%. That sounds low, but legal provisions are genuinely hard to classify — they are often multi-topic, taxonomies are imperfect, and even human experts sometimes disagree. The right way to use this pipeline is for aggregate analysis: asking "what share of AHKFTA's text covers Rules of Origin?" rather than trusting any individual provision's label. Aggregate distributions are robust even when individual labels are noisy.

The RAG-generated cross-agreement comparisons (see RAG Comparisons tab) are the most reliable output because the model reads actual retrieved text before writing, rather than relying on a single-shot label. Treat the individual classification labels as a triage tool, not a legal opinion.

Glossary of Abbreviations
FTA
Free Trade Agreement — a treaty reducing tariffs and trade barriers between countries.
RCEP
Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership — 15-party FTA (ASEAN + China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, New Zealand).
AHKFTA
ASEAN–Hong Kong Free Trade Agreement — bilateral goods-focused FTA, in force 2019.
AANZFTA
ASEAN–Australia–New Zealand FTA — comprehensive FTA including services and investment, signed 2009.
ASEAN
Association of Southeast Asian Nations — 10-country regional bloc (Indonesia, Thailand, Vietnam, Singapore, etc.).
LLM
Large Language Model — an AI model trained on text that can read, classify, and generate natural language. LLaMA and Qwen are both LLMs.
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation — relevant text is retrieved from a database and given to the LLM before it responds, improving accuracy.
RoO / Rules of Origin
Rules determining whether a product qualifies as "originating" from a country and thus eligible for preferential tariff rates.
RVC
Regional Value Content — a product qualifies if at least X% of its value was added within the FTA region. All three agreements use 40%.
CTC
Change in Tariff Classification — inputs must be classified under a different tariff code than the finished product, proving transformation occurred.
CTH
Change in Tariff Heading — CTC at the 4-digit HS level. Used by RCEP. Less strict than CC.
CC
Change in Chapter — CTC at the 2-digit HS level. Used by AHKFTA. Stricter — requires more substantial transformation.
HS Code
Harmonized System code — international numbering for traded goods. 2-digit = chapter, 4-digit = heading, 6-digit = subheading.
NTM
Non-Tariff Measure — trade barriers other than tariffs: import quotas, licensing requirements, technical standards.
SPS
Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures — rules protecting human, animal, and plant health from food-borne risks and pests.
IP
Intellectual Property — patents, copyright, trademarks, and geographic indicators.
ISDS
Investor-State Dispute Settlement — allows foreign investors to bring claims against host governments in international arbitration.
WTO DSU
WTO Dispute Settlement Understanding — the WTO's own dispute mechanism. AHKFTA references this instead of maintaining its own.
κ — Cohen's Kappa
Agreement between two classifiers, corrected for chance. 1.0 = perfect, 0 = chance, negative = worse than chance.
Macro-F1
Classification accuracy averaged equally across all categories, including rare ones. More informative than plain accuracy when categories are imbalanced.
Zero-shot
Prompt strategy with no examples — model uses only its training knowledge.
Few-shot
Prompt strategy with two labelled examples shown before the new provision.
CoT (Chain-of-Thought)
Prompt strategy instructing the model to reason step-by-step before answering. Helps Qwen, but hurts LLaMA on this task.
OCR
Optical Character Recognition — converts scanned page images into machine-readable text. Used as a fallback for scanned PDFs.

What This Means for Reliability

With κ values in the 0.58–0.70 range, the two models are substantially consistent with each other when working on the same provisions. They are not labelling at random — they share a meaningful common signal.

The most important finding here is that the aggregate category distributions are more trustworthy than individual labels. If both LLaMA and Qwen (zero-shot) assign 48% of AHKFTA provisions to Rules of Origin, that pattern is robust. The value of this pipeline is in answering questions like "what topics does AHKFTA focus on?" — not "is this exact provision a Rules of Origin provision?"

The highest within-model κ is for zero-shot pairs, and the highest cross-model κ is also between zero-shot runs. This is the practical recommendation: zero-shot produces the most reproducible and cross-model-consistent results, even if Qwen CoT edges it out on Macro-F1.

Three Structural Patterns Worth Highlighting
AHKFTA
Rules of Origin heavy — 48% of its sample, vs 24% for RCEP
Because AHKFTA is a goods-only agreement, its entire provision space is compressed into the chapters that goods-trade agreements include. Nearly half of all that space goes to Rules of Origin — the most rule-intensive area of any goods FTA. It also has the stricter CC transformation rule, generating more legal text per topic than RCEP's CTH.
AANZFTA
Dispute Settlement leader — 22 provisions vs 6 for RCEP and 0 for AHKFTA
AANZFTA maintains its own independent dispute settlement mechanism with dedicated adjudication and arbitration procedures. AHKFTA delegates entirely to the WTO DSU — no own chapter. RCEP has a mechanism but fewer provisions appear in the stratified sample.
RCEP
Broadest coverage — the only agreement with meaningful IP provisions (12)
RCEP's 15-party scope forces it to codify a wider range of policies. Its 20 Trade in Services provisions and 12 IP provisions reflect chapters that AHKFTA simply does not contain. RCEP is the only agreement of the three that functions as a comprehensive integration framework rather than a focused goods-trade instrument.

What Convergence and Fragmentation Mean for Policy

Convergent: Customs Procedures (1.00). All three agreements allocate almost identical shares of their text to customs procedures. This reflects the WTO Trade Facilitation Agreement providing a shared procedural baseline — documentation, advance rulings, risk-based release — that all three parties follow. This is an area where regional harmonisation has genuinely occurred.

Fragmented: Dispute Settlement (0.47) and Intellectual Property (low). These are driven almost entirely by AHKFTA having zero provisions in both categories. This is not a policy disagreement — RCEP and AANZFTA do not write their dispute chapters in incompatible ways. The fragmentation is a structural scope difference: AHKFTA simply does not include these chapters. An entropy-based metric cannot distinguish "disagree" from "absent," so this must be interpreted carefully.

Caveat on Rules of Origin. Rules of Origin appears convergent (high entropy ratio) but this is partly inflated by the few-shot classification method used. Both in-context examples in the prompt were goods-trade categories, which biases the model toward classifying more provisions as Rules of Origin across all three agreements — making the distribution appear more even than it actually is.

The Critical Compliance Finding — CC vs CTH

RCEP and AHKFTA both use a 40% RVC threshold. An exporter looking at just that number would conclude they meet the Rules of Origin requirements under both agreements. But the CTC rule — the other requirement — differs in a practically important way:

RCEP — CTH (Heading level, 4-digit)
The inputs and the finished product must fall under different 4-digit HS headings. You are allowed to use inputs from the same 2-digit chapter, as long as they end up at a different heading. Easier to satisfy — more flexible for manufacturers.
AHKFTA — CC (Chapter level, 2-digit)
The inputs and the finished product must fall under different 2-digit HS chapters. This is a much stricter test — it requires the manufacturing process to involve a genuine change in the nature of the goods at the chapter level.
Practical Impact
The same goods can qualify for RCEP preferential rates but fail AHKFTA's Rules of Origin test — even with identical 40% RVC compliance. An exporter who structures their supply chain for RCEP eligibility cannot assume AHKFTA coverage. This is the most immediately actionable finding in the project for compliance professionals.
