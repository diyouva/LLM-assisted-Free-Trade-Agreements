Implication for Policy Use

A cross-model κ of −0.02 between LLaMA few-shot and Qwen few-shot means the two models are labelling provisions independently of each other. Neither can serve as a ground truth for the other.

This has a critical implication: individual provision labels are noisy and should not be used for compliance-grade decisions. The correct application is aggregate distribution analysis — asking "how many provisions in AHKFTA address Rules of Origin?" rather than "is this specific provision a Rules of Origin provision?"

The highest κ (0.51) is within-model (LLaMA zero-shot vs. few-shot), confirming that prompt strategy consistency matters more than model size for reproducibility.

Policy Interpretation

Convergent areas (Customs Procedures, Other/Admin) suggest a shared regional template is emerging — likely influenced by WTO rules and prior ASEAN FTA experience. These are lower-stakes areas where harmonisation is easier.

Fragmented areas (Rules of Origin, Trade in Services, Dispute Settlement) are where each agreement follows a distinct design. These are also the areas exporters and compliance officers care most about — confirming that the "spaghetti bowl" problem is concentrated precisely where it matters most.

AHKFTA's zero count in Trade in Services, Dispute Settlement, and IP categories drives the fragmentation signal for those domains — it's not policy disagreement, it's structural scope difference.

Critical Compliance Finding: CC vs CTH

RCEP and AHKFTA both use a 40% Regional Value Content (RVC) threshold — the ASEAN standard. However, they differ on the Change in Tariff Classification (CTC) rule:

RCEP — CTH (Heading level)
Requires change at the 4-digit HS heading level. Allows transformation within the same chapter (2 digits). Easier to satisfy.
AHKFTA — CC (Chapter level)
Requires change at the 2-digit HS chapter level. Demands more substantial transformation of goods. Harder to satisfy.

Practical impact: The same goods can qualify for preferential treatment under RCEP but not AHKFTA — a real compliance risk for exporters operating under both agreements simultaneously.
