About This Project

What this tool does, why it exists, and how to navigate the results.

The Problem

The Asia-Pacific region has dozens of overlapping Free Trade Agreements (FTAs). Each agreement contains thousands of legal provisions covering topics like tariffs, customs rules, investment protections, and dispute resolution. For a trade analyst, a customs officer, or a negotiator, understanding how three different agreements treat the same topic — say, Rules of Origin — normally requires days of manual reading and comparison.

This project automates that comparison using Large Language Models (LLMs). It reads the full text of three ASEAN-centred FTAs, breaks each into individual provisions, classifies each provision into a policy category, and then generates structured cross-agreement comparisons.

The Three Agreements

RCEP (Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership) is the world's largest FTA by trade volume, covering 15 countries including China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and New Zealand alongside the 10 ASEAN members. Signed in 2020, it is comprehensive — it includes chapters on services, investment, intellectual property, and dispute settlement alongside goods-trade rules.

AHKFTA (ASEAN–Hong Kong Free Trade Agreement) was signed in 2017 and covers goods trade between ASEAN and Hong Kong. It is narrower by design — it does not include dedicated chapters on services, investment, or intellectual property, and it references the WTO's dispute settlement system rather than maintaining its own mechanism.

AANZFTA (ASEAN–Australia–New Zealand Free Trade Agreement) was signed in 2009 and is broadly comparable in scope to RCEP, including dedicated investment and dispute settlement chapters. It is notable for delegating many specific tariff thresholds to annexes rather than embedding them in the main text.

How the Pipeline Works

The system starts by extracting raw text from the agreement PDFs using a three-step fallback chain: first a layout-aware PDF reader (pdfplumber), then a lower-level text extractor (PyMuPDF), and finally Optical Character Recognition (OCR) for scanned pages. Each page of text is then split into individual provisions — the smallest meaningful legal unit, typically one article or paragraph.

Each provision is classified into one of 11 policy categories by an LLM. Two models are tested — LLaMA 3.3 70B and Qwen 3 32B — each under three prompt strategies: zero-shot (just the task), few-shot (two labelled examples), and chain-of-thought (step-by-step reasoning). This gives six classification runs. A stratified sample of 100 provisions per agreement (300 total) is used for the comparative analysis to prevent the larger RCEP corpus from dominating results.

For cross-agreement comparison, the system uses Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): it retrieves the most relevant provisions from each agreement for a given policy topic, then asks the LLM to write a structured comparison. The accuracy of the classifications is measured against a hand-labelled set of 50 provisions.

What the Numbers Mean

The best classification accuracy in this project is 48%, meaning roughly half of individual provision labels match the human-assigned label. This sounds low, but it is consistent with the difficulty of the task: legal provisions are often multi-topic, taxonomies are imperfect, and even human experts can disagree. The recommended use is for aggregate analysis — asking "how many AHKFTA provisions cover Rules of Origin?" rather than relying on any single provision's label. The RAG-based cross-agreement comparisons are more reliable than individual classifications, since they are based on retrieved text rather than single-shot labelling.

Glossary of Abbreviations
FTA
Free Trade Agreement — a treaty between countries that reduces or eliminates tariffs and other trade barriers.
RCEP
Regional Comprehensive Economic Partnership — a 15-party FTA covering 10 ASEAN nations plus China, Japan, South Korea, Australia, and New Zealand.
AHKFTA
ASEAN–Hong Kong Free Trade Agreement — a bilateral goods-focused FTA between ASEAN and Hong Kong, in force since 2019.
AANZFTA
ASEAN–Australia–New Zealand Free Trade Agreement — a comprehensive FTA including services, investment, and dispute settlement, signed 2009.
ASEAN
Association of Southeast Asian Nations — a 10-country regional bloc including Indonesia, Thailand, Vietnam, Singapore, and others.
LLM
Large Language Model — an AI model trained on text that can read, classify, and generate natural language. LLaMA and Qwen are both LLMs.
RAG
Retrieval-Augmented Generation — a technique where relevant text is retrieved from a database and given to an LLM before it generates a response, improving accuracy.
RoO / Rules of Origin
Rules that determine whether a product qualifies as "originating" from a country and therefore eligible for preferential tariff rates under an FTA.
RVC
Regional Value Content — one way to satisfy Rules of Origin: a product qualifies if at least X% of its value was added within the FTA region. All three agreements use a 40% threshold.
CTC
Change in Tariff Classification — an alternative way to satisfy Rules of Origin: inputs must be classified under a different tariff code than the final product, proving transformation occurred.
CTH
Change in Tariff Heading — a CTC rule requiring a change at the 4-digit HS code level. Used by RCEP. Less strict than CC.
CC
Change in Chapter — a CTC rule requiring a change at the 2-digit HS code level. Used by AHKFTA. Stricter than CTH, requiring more substantial transformation.
HS Code
Harmonized System code — an internationally standardised numbering system for traded goods. 2-digit codes are "chapters", 4-digit codes are "headings", 6-digit codes are "subheadings".
NTM
Non-Tariff Measure — trade barriers other than tariffs, such as import quotas, licensing requirements, and technical standards.
SPS
Sanitary and Phytosanitary Measures — rules protecting human, animal, and plant health from food-borne risks, diseases, and pests.
IP
Intellectual Property — legal rights covering inventions (patents), creative works (copyright), brand names (trademarks), and geographic indicators.
ISDS
Investor-State Dispute Settlement — a mechanism allowing foreign investors to bring claims directly against a host government in international arbitration.
WTO DSU
World Trade Organization Dispute Settlement Understanding — the WTO's own mechanism for resolving trade disputes. AHKFTA references this instead of maintaining its own mechanism.
κ (Cohen's Kappa)
A statistical measure of agreement between two classifiers that corrects for chance. 1.0 = perfect agreement, 0 = no better than chance, negative = worse than chance.
Macro-F1
Macro-averaged F1 score — measures classification accuracy across all categories equally, even rare ones. More informative than plain accuracy when categories are imbalanced.
Zero-shot
A prompt strategy where the model is given only the task description and provision text — no examples. The model must rely entirely on its training.
Few-shot
A prompt strategy where two labelled examples are shown to the model before the new provision, helping it understand the expected format and logic.
CoT (Chain-of-Thought)
A prompt strategy that instructs the model to reason step-by-step before giving its final answer. Improves accuracy on complex tasks — but not equally for every model.
OCR
Optical Character Recognition — software that converts scanned images of text into machine-readable characters. Used as a fallback for scanned PDF pages.
WTO / UNCTAD
World Trade Organization / United Nations Conference on Trade and Development — the two international bodies whose FTA chapter taxonomy this project's 11 categories follow.

Inter-Run Agreement (Cohen's κ)

How consistently do two different runs assign the same label to the same provision?

How to read this page
Each row in the table compares two classification runs on the provisions they both labelled. The κ (kappa) score tells you how much they agreed, after adjusting for the agreement you would expect from pure chance. A score of 1.0 means they agreed on every provision. A score of 0 means their agreement was no better than random guessing. A negative score — which appears in this dataset — means they disagreed more than chance would predict, which is a sign the two runs are labelling provisions based on very different signals. Green numbers indicate substantial agreement; yellow indicates fair to moderate agreement; red indicates near-random or worse-than-chance.

Implication for Policy Use

Once runs are compared on the exact same set of provisions, cross-model agreement reaches moderate to substantial levels (κ = 0.58–0.70). This is considerably better than near-random, and it means the models are largely reading the same signals in the text.

The highest agreement is cross-model zero-shot (κ = 0.702) — LLaMA and Qwen, without any examples or reasoning prompts, agree more with each other than any same-model pair using few-shot. This suggests that both models share a broadly similar prior understanding of FTA legal categories, and that prompting strategies disrupt this natural alignment more than they help.

The practical implication: aggregate category distributions are more defensible than individual provision labels. If two independent models consistently assign 48% of AHKFTA provisions to Rules of Origin, that aggregate finding is robust even if any single provision's label may be wrong.

Convergence Analysis

Which policy areas are treated similarly across all three agreements, and which diverge?

How to read this page
This chart asks a specific question for each policy category: are the three agreements giving it roughly the same amount of attention, or is one agreement dominating? The score on the x-axis runs from 0 to 1. A score near 1.0 means all three agreements allocate almost exactly the same share of their text to that category — they converge. A score near 0 means one agreement has almost all the provisions in that category and the others have almost none — they fragment. Green bars are convergent; orange bars are fragmented. Policy Interpretation

Convergent areas (Customs Procedures, Investment, Rules of Origin) suggest all three agreements allocate comparable shares of their text to these topics. For Customs Procedures, this reflects the shared WTO Trade Facilitation Agreement baseline that all three parties follow. For Rules of Origin, the convergence is partly inflated by a bias in the classification method — the two labelled examples used in the few-shot prompt were both goods-trade categories, which pushes the model to classify more provisions as Rules of Origin across all agreements.

Fragmented areas (Dispute Settlement, Intellectual Property, Sanitary and Phytosanitary) are where one agreement has most of the provisions and others have almost none. This is driven almost entirely by AHKFTA having zero provisions in these categories — not because the agreements disagree on how to write these chapters, but because AHKFTA simply does not include those chapters at all. It is a scope difference, not a policy disagreement.

Critical Compliance Finding: CC vs CTH

RCEP and AHKFTA both use a 40% Regional Value Content (RVC) threshold — the ASEAN standard. However, they differ on the Change in Tariff Classification (CTC) rule:

RCEP — CTH (Heading level)
Requires change at the 4-digit HS heading level. Allows transformation within the same chapter (2 digits). Easier to satisfy.
AHKFTA — CC (Chapter level)
Requires change at the 2-digit HS chapter level. Demands more substantial transformation of goods. Harder to satisfy.

Practical impact: The same goods can qualify for preferential treatment under RCEP but not AHKFTA — a real compliance risk for exporters operating under both agreements simultaneously. A manufacturer sourcing inputs within the same HS chapter may clear RCEP's heading-level test but fail AHKFTA's chapter-level test, meaning they lose access to AHKFTA preferential rates despite meeting the RVC requirement.

RAG Cross-Agreement Comparisons

LLM-generated comparative analysis for each policy category, using retrieved provisions from all three agreements.

How to read this page
Each accordion item covers one policy category. Click the category name to expand it and read the AI-generated comparison. For each category, the analysis was produced by giving Qwen 3 32B the three most semantically relevant provisions from each agreement (nine provisions total) and asking it to write a structured comparison. The comparison identifies what all three agreements have in common, what makes each agreement distinctive, and what the practical implications are for businesses or policymakers. Because this is generated text, important conclusions should always be verified against the original agreement text before being used in a compliance or negotiation context.
